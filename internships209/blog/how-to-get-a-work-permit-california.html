How to Get a Work Permit in California: A Step-by-Step Guide for Teens (2026)

7 min read
March 3, 2026

Ready to start your first job? California requires a work permit for teens under 18 (and especially under 16). Here's exactly how to get one — in plain English, without the jargon.

Who Needs a Work Permit in California?

In California, you need a work permit if you're under 18 years old — period. But the rules are stricter if you're under 16.

Ages 14-15

If you're 14 or 15, you definitely need a work permit. And there are extra rules: you can work a maximum of 3 hours per school day and 8 hours on a non-school day. During school weeks, you're capped at 12 hours of work total. During school breaks or summer, you can work up to 8 hours a day and 40 hours per week.

Ages 16-17

You still need a work permit, even though the restrictions are looser. You can work up to 4 hours on a school day and 8 hours on a non-school day, with a maximum of 20 hours per school week. These limits protect your time for school while letting you earn and gain experience.

Age 18+

Once you turn 18, you no longer need a work permit. You can work full-time if you choose to.

Important: Some jobs (like hazardous work or entertainment) have their own stricter rules. But if you get a standard work permit, you're covered for most retail, food service, and office positions.

What a Work Permit Actually Is

A California work permit is officially called a "Statement of Intent to Employ Minor and Request for Work Permit" — it's California Department of Education Form B1-1. Don't let the name intimidate you; it's just a one-page form that says:

  • Your employer intends to hire you
  • Your parent/guardian gives permission
  • Your school gives the OK (and confirms you're meeting grade requirements)

Once your school approves it, you get the official work permit, which is Form B1-4: the "Permit to Employ and Work." That's what you give to your employer. It's proof that you're legally cleared to work in California at that age.

Think of it this way: the work permit protects you and your employer. Your employer knows they're hiring legally, and you know you're protected by California's youth labor laws.

Step-by-Step: How to Get Your Work Permit

1
Get a Job Offer First

You need a real job offer before you can get a permit. Your employer has to commit to hiring you and fill out part of the form. This isn't something you can get "just in case" — you need an actual job waiting.

2
Pick Up or Download the Form from Your School

Head to your school's main office or counselor's office. They have the form (B1-1) ready to go. Some schools let you download it from their website. Ask for the "Statement of Intent to Employ Minor" form if they need clarification.

3
Have Your Employer Fill Their Section

Give the form to your new employer. They fill out their section: company name, address, type of work, proposed start date, hours per day/week, and wages. They might ask questions — that's normal. Make sure they sign and date their section.

4
Get Your Parent/Guardian's Signature

Bring the form home. Your parent or guardian needs to read it, sign the parent/guardian section, and date it. They're not signing blind — they can see the job details, hours, and employer information. This is your parent confirming they're OK with you working.

5
Submit to Your School Counselor or Principal

Take the signed form back to your school's counselor or main office. They'll check that all sections are filled out correctly, verify your grades are OK (you can't get a permit if you're falling behind in school), and process it officially.

6
Receive Your Official Work Permit

Your school will issue the official permit (Form B1-4). This is the document your employer needs before you start work. Make a copy for your records and give the original to your employer.

How Long Does It Take?

Once you submit everything to your school, expect the work permit to be ready in 2–5 school days. It's usually faster, but schools can take a few days to process.

Timeline tip: Don't wait until your start date to begin this process. Start as soon as you have the job offer. If your start date is in two weeks, get the ball rolling now — you'll have plenty of time.

Pro tip: If your school is slow, follow up politely. Call or visit the counselor's office. Some schools prioritize faster processing if you ask. And if your employer is waiting, you can mention that too — schools understand you're eager to start.

What Your Employer Needs to Know

Your employer probably knows the basics, but here's what they're looking for:

  • The permit must be in hand before you start work. They can't let you start without it, even on a trial shift. California law is clear on this.
  • They need to keep it on file. Your employer is required to keep a copy of your work permit during your employment. It's for their protection and yours.
  • It's tied to your hours. If the permit says you can work 15 hours per week, that's what they can schedule you for. They can't exceed those limits.
  • It's not transferable. If you switch jobs, you need a new permit for the new employer.

Most employers are used to this process. But if your employer seems confused, you can have them visit your school's office or contact the California Department of Education — they'll get their questions answered.

What About Summer Work? (And Other Special Cases)

Summer work is allowed and encouraged. Your work permit is technically tied to the school year, but you can absolutely work during summer break. The hours are more generous: you can work up to 8 hours a day and 40 hours a week (instead of the school-year limits).

When summer ends and school starts again, your original permit's hour restrictions go back into effect. So if you picked up summer work at the same employer, just make sure both you and your employer understand the schedule will change once fall classes begin.

If You Transfer Schools

Moving to a new high school mid-year? You'll need a new work permit from your new school. The process is the same, but get it sorted out early. Don't assume your old school's permit works at the new school — it doesn't.

If You're Homeschooled

Homeschooled students still need a work permit. Contact your local school district office — they'll tell you who to submit the form to. It might be the district office itself rather than a specific school.

Resources & Next Steps

You can also download the official forms directly:

  • Form B1-4: Permit to Employ and Work (what you get back after approval)

Both are available on the California Department of Education website, but honestly, your school office will have them ready for you.
